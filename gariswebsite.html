THE GARIS
COMPETITION

GARIS (Global Association of Research & Innovation for Students) is a student-led competition created to provide opportunities for students to develop and communicate original scientific work through research. “GARIS,” meaning “line,” reflects our vision to empower the next generation around the globe to redefine the lines of youth engagement in research and innovation.

Participants may work individually or in teams of 1–7 in the Research track. The programme supports teams through structured milestones and skill-building sessions with mentors and guest speakers.

JUDGING CRITERIA

Research submissions are evaluated using a structured rubric (Total: 90 points), organised into five sections (18 points each). Judges use level descriptors to assess clarity, rigour, evidence quality, originality, and academic execution. Academic integrity applies: all work must be original, all sources must be cited, and any use of generative AI must be disclosed.

Research Question & Design (18)

Problem definition & scope; research question clarity; alignment between objectives and approach.

Methodology & Process (18)

Method selection and suitability; procedural detail & replicability; feasibility and resource use (if applicable).

Evidence & Citation Practice (18)

Source quality and relevance; citation accuracy and consistency; integration of evidence into argumentation.

Originality & Contribution (18)

Analytical depth and synthesis; innovation/differentiation; broader implications and why the work matters.

Execution & Academic Quality (18)

Organization and argument flow; academic language and terminology; conclusions grounded in evidence with limitations.

Integrity & AI Disclosure

Plagiarism is prohibited (text/data/images/code without attribution, fabrication, etc.). Submissions are screened with plagiarism detection; similarity index must not exceed 30% (excluding properly quoted and cited material). Any use of generative AI must be explicitly disclosed, and students remain responsible for accuracy and integrity.

RESOURCES

This page compiles programme-aligned guidance for research writing, documentation, validation, and academic integrity. If you want the full source list, see the external journals/guidelines used for reference in our planning notes (examples included below).

Research

Submission Format

File format: single PDF. Use a clear filename convention (e.g., TeamName_Research_GARIS2026.pdf). Supplementary materials (optional): appendices, raw data files, or code to support reproducibility.

Structure

Required Paper Sections

Title Page; Abstract (150–250 words); Introduction; Literature Review; Methods; Results; Discussion; Conclusion; References (APA 7). Every in-text citation must appear in the reference list, and vice versa.

Eligibility

Allowed Project Types

Analytical, computational, theoretical, modelling, simulation, data-driven, or systematic review projects only. No wet-lab. No human/animal subjects. No clinical testing.

Formatting

Formatting & Layout

Use a standard 12-point font (Times New Roman, Arial, or similar). 1-inch (2.54 cm) margins. Clear headings. Label tables/figures and reference them in the text before they appear.

Integrity

Plagiarism & AI Rules

All work must be original. Cite sources whenever you quote, paraphrase, reference data, or reuse tables/figures. Generative AI use must be disclosed; students remain fully responsible for accuracy and integrity.

JUDGES

Judges are the independent evaluators responsible for assessing all team submissions against standardized criteria and selecting the most promising work. Judges maintain the integrity, credibility, and fairness of the competition through objective evaluation and deliberation.

Judge Responsibilities
  • Submissions are anticipated to be due September 2026.
  • Judges will have a minimum of 4 weeks to finalize their evaluation.
  • Judges are expected to review approximately 10–15 submissions using the provided evaluation criteria.

GUEST SPEAKERS

Guest speakers are allocated a 40-minute session that can be in-person, online, or pre-recorded. Sessions may be centered on industry insights, topic-specific lectures (e.g., their own research and its implications), or practical workshops (e.g., research paper writing, scientific communication, research documentation, validation, and execution planning).

How Sessions Support Participants
  • Weekly Friday lunch sessions build specific skills and expose participants to a wide range of scientific fields.
  • Check-in & feedback sessions (with mentors) help teams revise plans and prepare for execution.
  • Milestone announcements keep teams aligned on deliverables and deadlines.

GUEST SPEAKERS

Guest speakers bring industry expertise, real-world experience, and inspiration to the competition. Sessions can be delivered in-person, online, or pre-recorded, and may include industry insights, topic lectures on speakers’ research and professional work, or practical workshops to strengthen student research deliverables.

INSTITUTIONAL
PARTNERSHIPS

PURPOSE OF PARTNERSHIPS
  • Provide students with access to real-world expertise, facilities, and problems, strengthening the authenticity and relevance of their projects.
  • Support workforce readiness by aligning learning experiences with current industry practices and emerging skills.
TYPES OF PARTNERS
  • Educational, industry, and nonprofit institutions for problem statements, domain expertise, internships, and sponsorship of research challenges.
POSSIBLE PARTNERSHIP ACTIVITIES
  • Co-designed project briefs or challenge statements that reflect real organisational needs or community issues.
  • Guest talks, mentorship clinics, and site visits that expose students to different professions and research pathways.
  • Joint showcases or conference-style sessions where partners give feedback, review papers, and identify promising work or talent.
